# Demystifying Time Concept

Why is time irreversible? Because every energy transfer increases multiplicative entropy (S = ∏ mᵢ), and reversing would require entropy decrease, which violates the fundamental energy gradient rule.  

How to understand time? Time is not a flow or dimension—it is the counting of discrete spatial transformations mapped to increasing entropy values.

To understand time, we must first understand the nature of physical phenomena. Within this paper's framework, physical phenomena are not performances happening on the stage of space—they are redistributions of energy within space itself as the ontological reality. Space is not a passive background, but the sole entity. All fields, particles, mass, and charge are manifestations of different excited states of space. Therefore, any change is a reconfiguration of energy distribution within space.

What drives this change is an extremely simple dynamical rule: energy can only flow from a Space Elementary Quantum carrying higher energy to an adjacent quantum carrying lower energy, transferring exactly one fundamental unit of Planck's constant each time. This is the energy gradient driving rule—temperature gradients, potential gradients, chemical potential gradients, and curvature gradients are all coarse-grained manifestations of this single rule at different scales.

So how do we characterize the "progression" of this energy redistribution process? The paper introduces a novel definition of entropy—multiplicative entropy. For a closed system of n Space Elementary Quanta, the entropy S equals the product of the energy values of all quanta: S = ∏ m_i. Unlike traditional Boltzmann entropy, which concerns only the number of macroscopic states, multiplicative entropy records the details of every microscopic energy transfer. More importantly, it naturally satisfies the Second Law of Thermodynamics: every legitimate energy transfer necessarily increases entropy. This means multiplicative entropy is not a static state function, but a "thermodynamic coordinate" that can trace the evolutionary path—just as latitude and longitude mark positions in space, multiplicative entropy marks the system's position along its evolutionary trajectory.

With this foundation in place, the definition of time becomes clear and direct: every spatial transformation (that is, every energy redistribution event) corresponds to a unique multiplicative entropy value, and the increasing sequence of entropy values constitutes a partially ordered set of time. In other words, time is not some fluid flowing into the universe from outside, nor a pre-existing background parameter—time is the counting of spatial transformations. Specifically: one spatial transformation corresponds to one multiplicative entropy value corresponds to one possible moment. Global cosmic time is the cumulative count of transformations across the entire universe, while local time is the cumulative count of transformations within a specific spatial region.

This definition resolves several key problems of time at once. The directionality of time: since every legitimate transformation increases multiplicative entropy, the flow of time necessarily points toward increasing entropy. This is the microscopic origin of the thermodynamic arrow of time—not an additional assumption, but a mathematical consequence of the energy gradient driving rule. The irreversibility of time: if time were merely the counting of transformations, then "reversal" would mean decreasing entropy, but the multiplicative entropy formula proves that any reverse operation would decrease the product, violating the basic rule. Thus, the irreversibility of time is not a regret of the universe, but a natural consequence of the logic of energy redistribution.

The nature of time dilation: in general relativity, time slows down near strong gravitational fields. This model's explanation is remarkably straightforward—spatial deformation (whether compression or stretching) modulates the resonant frequency of Space Elementary Quanta. A change in frequency means the number of state transitions completed per unit time changes accordingly. A clock runs slow not because of some mysterious spacetime warping, but because the space quanta where the clock resides have their resonant frequencies modulated by spatial deformation, causing their "update rate" to change. Different deformation modes produce asymmetric modulation magnitudes, but the fundamental mechanism is clear: geometric deformation modulates frequency, frequency determines transition rate, and transition rate is precisely the rate at which time flows.

The discreteness of time: since energy transfer occurs in fundamental units of Planck's constant, and each transfer corresponds to a minimal Planck time interval, time is naturally discrete. The continuous time we perceive is a smooth approximation of vast numbers of discrete events at the macroscopic scale.

In summary, time is not an independently existing entity, nor is it an illusion—it is the ordering relation exhibited by space, the ontological reality, in its process of energy redistribution. The key to understanding time is not inventing more precise clocks, but understanding how space rearranges its energy, and how spatial geometry modulates resonant frequencies to control the tempo of transformation.
